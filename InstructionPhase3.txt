Lab Session: Knowledge reasoning with rule and
Knowledge Graph Embedding (KGE)
(Using the Knowledge Base Constructed in the Previous Lab)
Objective
In this lab, you will firstly apply SWRL to design reasoning rules on a prepared ontology.
Then, you will apply Knowledge Graph Embedding (KGE) techniques to the knowledge base constructed
and expanded in the previous session.
You will:
Prepare the KB for embedding
Train embedding models
Evaluate link prediction performance
Analyze the impact of KB structure and size
Compare different embedding approaches
The quality of your embeddings will strongly depend on the design decisions made during the previous lab.
You may need 3H to 6H to do this lab.
Part 1 - Knowledge reasoning with SWRL
Load file family.owl and apply a SWRL rule infering "a person who is older than 60 years old is an
'oldPerson'" ('oldPerson' is a class).
Install OWLReady2 as the reasoning engine in Python.
Mark your SWRL rule and the reasoning result.
You will also need to use this in Exercise 8 of Part 2.
Part 2 - Knowledge Graph Embedding
1 — Data Preparation
1.1 Input
Use the expanded KB generated in the previous lab:
(The reason of the volume: If the entity number is too small, the embedding make no sense. If the number is
too big, it would be hard to train on your personal computers.)
50,000 – 200,000 triplets
5,000 – 30,000 entities
50 – 200 relations
1.2 Cleaning for Embedding
Before training:
Remove duplicate triplets
Remove inconsistent URIs
Ensure all entities and relations are uniquely indexed
Optionally remove literal-heavy predicates
1.3 Train / Validation / Test Split
Split the dataset:
80% Training
10% Validation
10% Test
Ensure:
No entity appears only in validation/test
No leakage between splits
Deliverable:
train.txt
valid.txt
test.txt
2 — Embedding Models
You must implement or use at least two embedding models.
Recommended options:
TransE
DistMult
ComplEx
RotatE
You may use frameworks such as:
PyKEEN (recommended)
or other frameworks: DGL-KE, OpenKE or Custom PyTorch implementation
Specify:
Embedding dimension (e.g., 100 or 200)
Learning rate
Batch size
Number of epochs
Negative sampling strategy
Keep configuration consistent across models for fair comparison.
4 — Evaluation Task: Link Prediction
Evaluate models using:
Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR)
Hits@1
Hits@3
Hits@10
Perform both:
Head prediction
Tail prediction
Report filtered metrics.

With your best trained embedding model, do the following analysis:

6.2 Clustering analysis
After training your KGE model:
. Extract entity embeddings

Color the entities according to their ontology class, and answer the following question:
Do entities of the same class cluster together?
Does the embedding capture semantic structure?

7 — Critical Reflection
Discuss:

Explain how the previous lab design influenced embedding performance.
8 - Comparison between rule-based and embedding
based reasoning
Design a SWRL rule for your knowledge base (don't make it too complicated, a horn rule with two
conditions is sufficient) Can you find a similar result with your embedding result? Example: for rule
Person(?p) ∧ hasSibling(?p,?s) ∧ Man(?s) → hasBrother(?p,?s) Can you find a result
giving vector(hasSibling)+vector(Man)$\approx$ vector(hasBrother)?

Expected Observations
Small KBs produce unstable embeddings
TransE struggles with complex relations
DistMult fails on antisymmetric relations
ComplEx handles asymmetric relations better
Larger KB improves stability but increases training time.
Learning Outcomes
After this lab, you should understand:
How KGE models learn structure
How KB quality impacts embeddings
Differences between translational and bilinear models
Practical limitations of KGE on real data
Important
Your results will be highly dependent on:
KB size
Predicate alignment correctness
Graph connectivity
Noise introduced during expansion
A poorly constructed KB will lead to poor embeddings.
